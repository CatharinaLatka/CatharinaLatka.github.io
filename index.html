Catharina Latka

Researcher in food and resource economics

I am a postdoctoral researcher at Princeton University's High Meadows Environmental Institute and I am affiliated with the Environmental Markets Lab (emLab) at University of California, Santa Barbara. My research interest lies at the intersection of food systems and the environment. I study how policies and price shocks affect food security and environmental outcomes, identifying synergies and tradeoffs. In my research I apply econometric and simulation modelling techniques primarily using R. You can contact me per email under clatka[at]ucsb.edu

Policy design for efficient and equitable land-based climate solutions

The design of forest conservation policies matters for their environmental and welfare impacts, cost-effectiveness, and distributional consequences. We use global, spatially-granular data to assess different policies that seek emissions reductions from avoided deforestation. Under a subsidy regime, information asymmetries impose substantial costs. Some land users are unlikely to participate, whereas others are paid for partly non-additional emission savings. Carbon taxes and deforestation bans do not require similar targeting efforts. Monetary transfers could mitigate related equity concerns.

Local unexpected food price volatility affects children’s nutrition in sub-Saharan Africa

Food access can be affected by high and volatile food prices with potentially severe consequences for children’s nutrition. Local food price volatility is driven by international prices and local climate shocks. We decompose local maize price movements across 508 markets in 24 sub- Saharan African (SSA) countries to understand the sources of variation using econometric approaches. Next, we compile DHS data over 19 years to measure how food price volatility affects children’s nutrition in SSA.
